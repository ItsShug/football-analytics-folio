What Kind of Champions Are Arsenal? A Data-Driven Look at Their Title-Winning Season

Arsenal have ended their 21-year wait for a Premier League title. But beyond the celebrations, what does the data actually tell us about the kind of champions they are? We've benchmarked their 2025/26 campaign against every title-winning side since Chelsea's 2014/15 season to find out where Mikel Arteta's side stand historically — and where the gaps still exist.

Built on Defensive Solidity

If there is one area where Arsenal's title credentials are beyond question, it is at the back. Their defensive profile this season sits clearly above the Premier League champion average across nearly every metric. They have conceded fewer goals per game, limited opponents to less threatening chances, and kept more clean sheets than the typical title winner. That is a remarkable achievement, and it is the bedrock upon which this championship has been built.

Defensive Profile — Arsenal 2025/26 vs PL Champion Average Source: FBref / StatsBomb
Arsenal sit above the champion average across every defensive metric shown. The gap is particularly pronounced for goals conceded and clean sheets, underlining just how dominant their backline has been this season.

Dig into the breakdown of goals conceded by situation, and the picture gets even more impressive. Their 20 open play goals conceded is the lowest in the dataset, comfortably below the champion average of 22. They have been equally disciplined from set pieces and corners, giving very little away from dead ball situations. If you are looking for a side that has been hard to break down in every sense, Arsenal are it.

Premier League Champions — Goals Conceded by Situation Source: FBref / StatsBomb
Arsenal's 20 open play goals conceded is the lowest in the dataset. Their discipline from set pieces and corners further underlines a defensive record that stands alone among title-winning sides since 2014/15.

The Attacking Question

Here is where it gets more interesting. Placed against the champion average on attacking output, Arsenal fall short in a number of areas. Their shot volume, non-penalty xG, xG chain involvement, and key passes are all below what previous title-winning sides have typically produced. In short, they have not been as expansive or as prolific going forward as the benchmark suggests champions usually are.

Attacking Output — PL Champion Average vs Arsenal 2025/26 Source: FBref / StatsBomb
Arsenal trail the champion average in shot volume, non-penalty xG, and xG chain involvement. The gap is consistent rather than concentrated in one area, pointing to a structural difference in attacking approach rather than a single missing ingredient.

The goals by situation data reinforces this. Arsenal's 42 open play goals is the lowest in the dataset, and their total goal output is well below the historical average for title winners. Notably, 16 of their goals came from corners, which is the highest corner contribution in the entire dataset. That is not a coincidence. It speaks to a deliberate and well-drilled approach to set pieces that has compensated for a relative lack of open play volume going forward.

Key Finding
16 corner goals
Arsenal scored more goals from corners than any other champion in the dataset since 2014/15 — a deliberate set-piece system that has filled the gap left by modest open play output.
Premier League Champions — Goals by Situation Source: FBref / StatsBomb
Arsenal's open play total of 42 is the lowest in the dataset. Their corner tally of 16 stands out at the opposite extreme — the highest recorded for any side in this period.

The goals by position breakdown tells a similar story. Their forwards have contributed fewer goals than the champion average, and their midfielders are below par on that measure too. Arsenal have won this title without a traditional goal-machine leading the line, which makes the achievement all the more tactically fascinating.

Premier League Champions — Goals by Position Source: FBref / StatsBomb
Arsenal's forwards and midfielders both sit below the champion average for goal contributions. The title has been built on collective defensive output and set-piece efficiency rather than individual attacking brilliance.

Their Closest Historical Comparison

When we map Arsenal's profile against the most statistically similar champion in the dataset, the closest match is José Mourinho's Chelsea side of 2014/15. That Chelsea team was famously built on defensive organisation and game management rather than free-flowing attacking football. The comparison is apt. Both sides sit in a similar place for xGA and goals conceded, and both relied heavily on keeping opponents at bay rather than outscoring them.

Arsenal 2025/26 vs Closest Historical Champion — Chelsea 2014/15 Source: FBref / StatsBomb
The two sides track closely across xGA, goals conceded, and attacking creation metrics. Arsenal edge Chelsea on clean sheets and total goals conceded, while their xG and xA figures suggest broadly comparable chance creation despite fewer open play goals.

Where Arsenal actually pull ahead of that Chelsea vintage is on clean sheets and goals conceded overall, suggesting this may be an even more defensively disciplined title win. The attacking metrics tell a more nuanced story, with the two sides tracking closely in most areas, though Arsenal's xG and xA figures suggest they have created chances at a broadly comparable rate despite scoring fewer open play goals.

What This Season Tells Us

Arsenal are champions built on structure, discipline, and a ruthless exploitation of set pieces. They are not the most prolific attacking side to have won this league, and compared to the champion average, there are areas where Arteta will know improvement is needed. But they have been remarkably consistent, incredibly hard to score against, and clinical enough in the moments that matter.

Bottom Line
20 open play goals conceded
The lowest figure in the dataset since 2014/15 — the defensive record that defines this title win and sets the foundation for what comes next.

Whether this is the start of a sustained period of dominance or a single title win will depend on whether they can close the gap in attacking output next season. The foundation is clearly there. The question is what they build on top of it.

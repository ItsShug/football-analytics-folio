Did the 48-Team World Cup Actually Change Anything?

When FIFA expanded to 48 teams the debate split predictably. Dilution versus opportunity, mismatches versus new stories. Now that 2026 has given us actual data to look at, the picture is more interesting than either camp probably expected.

Huw Morgan 21 Jul 2026 5 min read

When FIFA confirmed the expansion from 32 to 48 teams, the debate split fairly predictably down the middle. One side worried about dilution; more mismatches, more one-sided group games, a tournament stretched thin to accommodate teams that wouldn't previously have been there. The other side pointed to opportunity: more nations, more stories, more countries with a genuine shot at a first appearance in decades.

Now that 2026 has given us actual data to look at, rather than just opinion, the picture is more interesting than either camp probably expected. In most of the ways that matter on the pitch, the tournament looks remarkably similar to its 32-team predecessor. The real shift is happening somewhere else entirely.

The football itself barely moved

Start with the most basic measure; how many goals are actually going in. Group stage scoring is up slightly, from an average of 2.50 goals per game across the 32-team era to 2.99 in 2026. That's a modest bump, and probably reflects a few more gentle group draws against sides with less top-level pairings. But knockout football, where it really counts, actually dipped a touch, from 2.67 down to 2.38.

Average Goals per Game — Group vs Knockout, by Era Source: API-Football · 2026 World Cup data
Group stage scoring ticks up modestly in 2026, while knockout football dips slightly. The two moves roughly cancel each other out — not the collapse in quality either camp predicted.

Nothing here suggests a tournament that's been watered down or turned into a goal fest. It's a swing in one direction at one stage and a swing the other way at the next, roughly cancelling out.

The story is much the same when you look at how one-sided matches actually are. The distribution of winning margins across the 48-team era sits almost perfectly on top of the historical curve from 1998 to 2022. Most matches are still decided by a single goal, blowouts of four or more remain rare, and the shape of the whole thing is close to identical.

World Cup — Distribution of Winning Margins Source: API-Football · 1998–2026
The 2026 distribution overlaps almost perfectly with the 32-team era. One-goal wins dominate; heavy defeats remain rare. No meaningful shift in competitive balance.

Group stage results tell the same story again. Win rates for the first-listed team sit at 47.5% in the old era and 47.2% now. Draws have ticked up marginally, from 26.2% to 27.8%, and losses have dipped from 26.3% to 25.0%. These are the kind of differences you'd expect from natural variation between tournaments, not evidence of a fundamentally different competition.

Group Stage — Match Outcomes by Era Source: API-Football · 1998–2026
Win rates, draw rates, and loss rates are effectively unchanged. The extra 16 teams have not altered the basic competitive character of the group stage.

So where has the format actually made a difference?

If goals, margins, and results have barely shifted, the obvious question is what expanding by 16 teams was actually for. The answer shows up clearly once you look at the quality gap between opponents.

The median FIFA ranking gap between group stage opponents has jumped from 59 points in the 32-team era to 80 in 2026. The spread has widened too; the top of the range now stretches well past 200, whereas it previously topped out closer to 185. More teams from outside the traditional top bracket means more mismatches on paper, at least in terms of ranking.

Group Stage — FIFA Ranking Gap Between Opponents Source: API-Football · FIFA Rankings · 1998–2026
The median ranking gap rises from 59 to 80 points, and the tail stretches further. The 48-team field genuinely is wider in quality terms, the data confirms what felt intuitive.

You'd expect that widening gap to translate into more shock results, and it does, just not dramatically. Upsets, defined as a lower-ranked side beating a higher-ranked one outright, have risen from 23.5 per 100 group matches to 27.8. That's a real increase, but it's nowhere near the surge you might picture if you assumed a bigger field would be full of hopeless mismatches.

Upset Rate - Lower-Ranked Team Wins per 100 Group Matches Source: API-Football · FIFA Rankings · 1998–2026
Upsets are up, from 23.5 to 27.8 per 100 matches. A meaningful shift, but not the wave of shock results that critics anticipated.

Put those last two charts together and the honest conclusion is this: the 48-team format has, as intended, brought in a wider spread of footballing quality. But that wider gap hasn't broken the tournament. Games are still competitive, results are still balanced, and the extra teams haven't turned the group stage into a procession of formalities.

A quieter kind of success

There's a version of this expansion that critics feared, group stages padded out with no-contest fixtures, knockout football diluted by teams who arguably shouldn't be there. The data doesn't really back that up. What we have instead is a tournament that plays out almost identically to its predecessor, with a modestly wider talent pool and a modestly higher chance of an upset.

And that wider pool is exactly what got Scotland back to a World Cup for the first time since 1998. Given how much that ranking gap has widened, and given upsets are now happening more often than they used to, a lower-ranked side making life difficult for a bigger name isn't just plausible. On this evidence, it's more likely than it's ever been.

Expected Goals and the Art of Missing: A Visual Guide to xG

Not all shots are equal. Expected goals models assign each attempt a probability based on location, angle, and context. Here's what the data actually tells us — and where the model quietly breaks down.

Your Name Here 15 Sep 2025 6 min read

The expected goals model was once the preserve of analytics consultants and StatsBomb subscribers. Now it appears on Match of the Day. But most coverage glosses over what xG actually measures — and, more importantly, what it doesn't.

At its core, xG is a probability. Every shot taken in a match is assigned a value between 0 and 1: the expected probability of a goal from that position, given historical data from thousands of similar attempts. A shot from six yards out, central, with a clear line of sight might carry an xG of 0.72. A 30-yard thunderbolt might clock in at 0.03.

Why location alone isn't enough

Early xG models used just two variables: distance from goal and shot angle. Modern versions layer in far more — whether the chance came from a cross, a through ball, or a set piece; the pressure on the shooter; their dominant foot; even the sequence of passes that created the opportunity.

came from shots with xG ≥ 0.10 — yet those shots made up only 31% of all attempts in the 2024/25 Premier League season.

The finishing debate

When a striker consistently outperforms their xG — scoring more than the model predicts — there are two explanations. Either they have genuinely elite finishing ability, or they've been fortunate. Separating signal from luck requires a large enough sample.

The uncomfortable truth is that for most players, a single season (roughly 80–120 shots) isn't sufficient. You typically need two or three seasons of data before over- or under-performance carries real statistical weight.

What xG can't tell you

Expected goals models are trained on historical shot data. They know how often shots like this one have gone in. They don't know the goalkeeper's exact positioning in this specific moment, whether a defender's outstretched leg slightly deflected the ball, or that the striker was carrying a hamstring knock since Thursday.

Reading xG sceptically

The goal isn't to replace watching football with reading spreadsheets. It's to sharpen your intuitions. When a pundit says a striker is "in great form," xG lets you ask: are they genuinely finding better positions, or are they in the same spots and finishing above expectation right now?

The model should make you ask better questions. It doesn't answer them.

Worth Remembering
~100 shots
is the minimum sample before a striker's xG outperformance carries meaningful statistical weight. Most single-season debates are premature.

Used well, expected goals is one of the most honest lenses available to a football analyst. It strips away narrative and forces you to confront probability. What you do with that probability — whether you contextualise it, interrogate it, or simply use it to ask harder questions — remains a very human decision.
